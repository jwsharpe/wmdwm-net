    The English language is estimated to hold roughly a million words, though that figure shifts as slang, jargon, and new coinages arrive. Major dictionaries paint a smaller picture of what is in active use: the Oxford English Dictionary, for example, lists about 171,476 "used currently". No one person carries the whole language in their head; by age 20, the average English speaker is estimated to know about 20,000 words, by 30 about 27,000, and by 60 about 32,000.

    We lean on a few thousand words day to day, recognise many more when we read or hear them, and leave the vast majority to references. Vocabulary is personal and uneven, you might know a word from work, from a region, or from a single memorable line, while never needing another ten thousand entries sitting quietly in the dictionary.
